600 years of Ahom Kingdom
by Supriya Dutta

Ahoms were the descendants of Shan or Tai race, who came to the Brahmaputra Valley of North East during 13th century. With glorious 600 years(1228-1824) they entail a very important part in the Assamese history.

The first king, Su-ka-pha came to Assam from Mungmao, currently in Yunan province of China. Sukapha on his way to the Brahmaputra valley of Assam, he conquered Khamjang area of Nagaland. He established his leadership by occupying Namrup, Tipam, areas between Disang and Dikhow River and areas occupied by Moran and Borahi communities. In 1253, he established his capital at Charaidau, Assam.

AHOM ADMINISTRATION SYSTEM
Ahom administration system had a monarchic structure. Swargadeo or the king was the head of the council. It was the administration system that played a crucial part their success of 600 years. Officers were given different powers and responsibilities. The administration system had a total of 162 officers. It was divided into two parts: Political and civil. Here we will discuss the political part of the administration system that shaped the 600 years of Ahoms.
Political part of the administration system had 26 officials, each with different powers and responsibilities. Here they are arranged according to the political hierarchy followed in the Ahom system.

EVOLUTION OF THE ADMINISTRATION SYSTEM
When Sukapha came to Assam he started the administration system with only two people. By the end of 600 years they had a total of 26 officers in the political division of the administration system. To see the evolution of the administration system, I decided to map the timeline of both the geographic area of the kingdom and the administration system. And here is what was observed.
Most of the expansion happened during Suhungmung’s reign. During his reign four officers were appointed. For better management of the kingdom Ahoms had a rule of dividing the kingdom into several territories, which were then allotted to different officers. Officers in the political division, along with their responsibilities were also allotted a piece of land to rule.
OFFICERS IN THE ADMINISTRATION SYSTEM
Officers in the administration system had to belong to certain groups to get elected as one. The most desirable person of the group was chosen for the positions in the administration system.

The visualization shows the Officials and the families that they belonged to. The most elegible member of those families were chosen for these positions.

When placed in the matrix, it was evident that most higher rank officers were from the Ahom community.

Only in the later part of their reign, when the administration system evolved, they started incorporating people from different community to the system. But as seen here, most of these people were elected only to the lower positions.

DID YOU NOTICE TWO INTERIM PERIODS || IN THE FIRST HALF?
It was another system very unique to Ahom at that time. There were five officers, namely: Borgohain, Buragohain, Barpatragohain, Barbarua, and Barpatragohain, who could directly engage themselves with the election of the king. Kings were primarily election through primogeniture, but could also be elected and depose by these five ministers. Let’s have a look.

In this later part of the timeperiod, we can see that, most kings were elected through ministers and deposed as well.

Around the timeperiod 1670-1680two of the Kings only had on 1 month and 20 days as their duration of reign

Ministers in the later part of the timeperiod started to exploit their powers repeatedly.

After King Sunyetpha or Udayadiyta, the kingdom started to grow weak internally and was subjected to numerous attacks from different groups.

The power given to the ministers to elect or depose kings was designed to better administer the kingdom. However, what started of as a noble power proved to be very fatal in the later part of Ahom’s history. This created instability in the administration system and could have been one of the possible reasons of their fall.

On 24 February, 1826, Assam was handed over to the British through an Yandabu Agreement, signed at Yandabu, near Burmese capital Ava.
